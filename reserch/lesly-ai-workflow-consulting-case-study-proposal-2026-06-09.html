Consulting case study draft 1

Local AI Visualization Pipeline for a Santa Cruz Area Residential Builder

Use this as an NDA-safe draft if the underlying facts are real and approved. Replace bracketed items before publishing.

Recommended public category: AI Workflow & Visual Pipeline Consulting

Turning inconsistent AI render experiments into a controlled local visualization workflow.

A small residential construction company serving the Santa Cruz and Monterey area needed faster, more consistent client-facing visuals for home builds, remodels, and material decisions. The team had tested online AI image tools, but the results drifted between runs, costs were hard to control, and the outputs did not respect the company's design language or material standards.

~40% reported reduction in AI visualization expense. Verify invoices and baseline before publishing.

Problem

  • Cloud AI tools were producing attractive but inconsistent visuals.
  • Prompts alone did not preserve building geometry, material choices, or client-approved direction.
  • The team lacked a shared material/design library, so every new image request started from scratch.
  • AI spend was rising without a repeatable workflow or clear acceptance criteria.

Lesly's role

  • Defined the art-direction and material consistency requirements.
  • Mapped the workflow from reference photos, drawings, and material boards into controlled image generation.
  • Worked with an agentic AI consulting/development partner to design the internal tool path.
  • Specified the visual library: materials, lighting moods, exterior/interior references, prompt templates, and review standards.

Workflow direction

  • Local ComfyUI / Stable Diffusion setup for private, repeatable generation.
  • ControlNet-style structure guidance from plans, sketches, depth/edge passes, or site photos.
  • LoRA or style-reference strategy for company-specific material language, if training data and rights are approved.
  • Material library for siding, stone, tile, wood, fixtures, paint, landscape, and lighting options.
  • Seed, prompt, reference, and output logging so approved looks could be reproduced.

Deliverables

  • Workflow map and tool recommendations.
  • Prompt and style guide for construction visualization.
  • Material/design library taxonomy.
  • Review checklist for geometry, material consistency, and client-ready output.
  • Implementation brief for developers or the AI consulting agency.

Proof to collect before publishing

Baseline cloud AI spend, post-implementation AI spend, workflow screenshots, material library screenshots, local hardware specs, model/tool list, sample before/after outputs, developer handoff notes, and approval to name or anonymize the builder.

Consulting case study draft 2

Client Progress Portal and Agentic Review Workflow for Residential Construction

This second case study makes Lesly's role broader than rendering: she helps the team design the client communication and feedback workflow around visual evidence.

Recommended public category: AI Workflow & Visual Pipeline Consulting

Designing a visual progress workflow where contractors, clients, and the agency could respond in one place.

The construction team needed a clearer way to document jobsite progress and communicate with homeowners. Photos, notes, and client feedback were scattered across texts, calls, emails, and ad hoc image-generation requests. Lesly helped define the visual workflow, review experience, and handoff requirements for an internal tool and client-facing portal.

1 place for progress photos, client comments, agency response, and visual review decisions.

Problem

  • Homeowners wanted visibility into progress without constant calls or texts.
  • Contractors captured jobsite photos, but the photos were not consistently structured for client review.
  • Client comments came through multiple channels, making it hard for the team or agency to respond cleanly.
  • AI-generated visuals were disconnected from real construction progress and material decisions.

Lesly's role

  • Defined the client review journey: progress photos, comments, approvals, and response loops.
  • Specified what contractors should capture on site: angles, milestones, materials, issues, and before/after states.
  • Outlined the comment model for text, audio, and video notes, plus agency responses.
  • Helped translate visual workflow requirements into an implementation brief for developers.

Workflow direction

  • Contractor captures site photos and optional voice/video notes by project phase.
  • AI assists with summaries, progress report drafts, and photo organization where safe.
  • Client reviews a personalized portal with progress photos, selections, comments, and status.
  • Agency or project team responds to comments with text/audio follow-up and visual clarification.
  • Approved materials and generated visuals are linked back to the design/material library.

Deliverables

  • Client portal experience map.
  • Photo capture and progress-report checklist.
  • Comment/response workflow for client and agency communication.
  • Visual standards for client-facing progress images.
  • Developer brief for internal tools and AI-assisted reporting.

Proof to collect before publishing

Portal screenshots, workflow diagram, sample progress report, sample client comment thread, capture checklist, developer tickets or implementation brief, before/after communication baseline, and approved client outcome metrics.

Publication rules

How to make these feel real without making unsupported claims.

Use this public wording

  • AI Workflow & Visual Pipeline Consulting
  • Local AI visualization workflow
  • Art-direction and material consistency system
  • Client progress portal workflow
  • Implementation brief for developers
  • Reported reduction in AI visualization expense, if verified

Do not publish until verified

  • Exact 40% savings number without invoices or before/after spend.
  • "Two Mac Studios combined into one GPU" without developer proof.
  • Client name, agency name, screenshots, or tool architecture without permission.
  • Claims that Lesly built the software herself if her role was strategy, art direction, workflow definition, and implementation coordination.
