# Senior 3D Portfolio Case-Study Benchmark

**Research date:** 2026-07-18

**Scope:** [Lesly's live homepage](https://leslyyankauskas.com/), all 13 public case-study routes, 18 primary-source artist case studies, the current portfolio case registry, and the current `build-lesly-case-study` workflow.

**Boundary:** Research and structural audit only. This report does not redesign or edit the website, change a skill, generate assets, publish work, or remove a case. Private client and proposal work, including OneOff, remains outside scope and untouched.

> **Audience correction — 2026-07-19:** The report's original recommendation to expose evidence status, unverified fields, proof gaps, and next-capture requests as public page content failed the recruiter-audience test. Those mechanics are internal review tools only. Public pages must use positive, supportable scope; omit unsupported claims; and tell a finished story about the brief, contribution, challenge, decisions, and visible result. Any later passage that recommends public evidence/proof-debt UI is superseded by this correction.

## Executive conclusion

Lesly's portfolio already has a visually coherent case set. Its central weakness is that internal truth-control language competes with the finished project stories hiring managers expect.

On the live case pages, a visitor first receives a large image, then later receives the title and project argument. Several cases use production-sounding titles—such as retopology, PBR texturing, progress workflow, or print direction—while the visible media primarily support character direction, look development, or reconstruction. The title and story must therefore narrow to the strongest supportable scope without publishing a warning about what is missing.

The benchmark shows that strong senior cases do four things quickly:

1. identify the project, artist's role, collaborators, constraints, and status;
2. show a strong final result without making the visitor decode the page;
3. select a small amount of proof that answers the professional question implied by the title;
4. make judgment visible through constraints, decisions, tradeoffs, credits, and outcomes—not through generic process volume.

For Lesly, the highest-leverage move is therefore to make every case project-story-led rather than template-led. Preserve all 13 case IDs and credible direction assets, but give each case a clear assignment, supportable contribution, one professional question, a meaningful decision, and a polished result. Where real DCC, client-review, engine, or print evidence does not exist, omit those claims and name the visible work positively as direction, development, or reconstruction. Do not manufacture technical proof from generated boards or publish the missing-proof checklist.

### Evidence labels used in this report

- **Visible:** directly observed on the live page or in a repository asset.
- **Claimed:** stated by the artist or project author on a primary-source page; not independently verified here.
- **Inferred:** a conclusion from the visible sequence or current site structure.
- **Recommended:** a proposed change, capture, or standard; not a statement about completed work.

### Method and limits

- The live homepage and every registered public case route were opened and visually inspected in the built-in browser. Supporting case media were also inspected at original resolution where necessary to judge what each image actually proves.
- External cases were opened on the artists' primary ArtStation pages. Visible page content and the artists' own captions or descriptions were kept separate through the evidence labels above.
- The current registry, case-study data, page structure, relevant portfolio guidance, and current case-building skill were read locally. No site or skill mutation was used to reach the findings.
- This is a case-story and evidence-architecture audit, not a full responsive implementation QA. Mobile behavior, runtime accessibility, and deployment integrity were not re-certified here.

### Benchmark synthesis

Across the strongest examples, seniority is conveyed less by page length than by selectivity and accountability. The visitor can usually answer five questions: What was the assignment? What did this artist own? What constraints mattered? What decisions changed the result? What visible evidence supports the case's headline?

The weaker patterns in the same sample are instructive. Some excellent artists publish beautiful galleries with almost no role or process context. Others publish very long tutorials whose volume obscures the portfolio signal. Neither model should be copied wholesale. Lesly needs the clarity and evidence linkage of the best cases, not their platform-specific page length.

## 2. Pattern matrix

| Pattern | Universal essential | Character / game | Props / interiors | Direction / client review | Print / collectible |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Immediate project identity | Title, one-line brief, status, role, ownership, and hero result in the first screen or first short scroll. | State whether this is concept, direction, sculpt, game-ready model, avatar, or shipped character. | State whether the work is one hero prop, a modular family, a set-dressing study, or a production scene. | State whether the work is speculative, internal, client-approved, implemented, or only a reconstructed workflow. | State whether the work is sculpture direction, engineered parts, test print, production master, or finished collectible. |

### Universal, conditional, and differentiating signals

- **Universal essentials:** project facts, status, role and ownership, strong representative result, professional question, decision sequence, proof matched to the title, credits, and an outcome or honest unresolved state.
- **Category-dependent proof:** topology and engine evidence for a game-ready claim; modularity and scene organization for an environment claim; review records for a workflow claim; engineering and physical output for a print-ready claim.
- **Senior differentiators:** candid constraints, rejected options, source lineage, ownership transitions, failure or revision evidence, and a clear reason for every included image.
- **Common weak signals:** long generic prose, future-tense production plans presented as process, unlabeled reconstruction, repeated views that answer no new question, and aggregate polish used as a substitute for category proof.

## 3. Universal case-study backbone

This is the recommended structural backbone for every Lesly case. It is intentionally shorter than the current repeated template. Category modules in section 4 add only the proof the case actually needs.

| Order | Section | Purpose | Recommended copy length | Expected visual or evidence |
|---:|---|---|---:|---|
| 1 | Hero + identity | Establish result, title, positive scope, supportable contribution, and assignment immediately. | Title + 12–25 word dek + 3–5 documented fact fields. | Strongest result with a concise presentation credit when needed. |
| 2 | Project facts | Clarify the documented brief, contribution, starting point, output, and credits without publishing unknown fields. | Compact labels; 40–90 words total. Unknown fields are omitted, never guessed or exposed as deficits. | No decorative image required. A small source or collaborator thumbnail is useful only when permissioned and credited. |
| 3 | The professional question | State the problem the case proves Lesly can solve. | One sentence, 20–40 words. | One supporting result or comparison if it clarifies the problem. |
| 4 | Constraint and decision | Show the most important constraint and the choice it produced. | 60–120 words or 2–3 annotated bullets. | One annotated comparison, rejected option, draw-over, source-to-decision, or before/after view. |
| 5 | Proof sequence | Answer the title's implied professional question with the minimum credible evidence. | 3–5 captions of 15–35 words each. | Category-specific proof. Every image must answer a named question; no filler beauty views. |
| 6 | Final result in context | Re-establish the selected output after the proof. | 20–60 words. | Final beauty, engine view, approved board, client delivery, or physical print. |
| 7 | Result and credits | Re-state the supportable finished result and documented collaborators or downstream owners. | 40–100 words. | Optional matching outcome artifact; otherwise the strongest presentation result. |
| 8 | Next relevant case + contact | Preserve the conversion path without diluting the case. | One short recommendation and one CTA. | Distinct related-case thumbnail; never duplicate the current hero. |

### The 10-second, 30-second, and deep-read contract

**In the first 10 seconds**, a visitor should see the result, case title, positive scope, Lesly's supportable contribution, and one sentence describing the assignment. On the current case template, the large hero is often the entire first viewport, so this information arrives late.

**By 30 seconds**, the visitor should understand the problem, the critical constraint, the decision Lesly made, and the strongest proof. A reader should not need to pass “Project direction,” “Problem solved,” and a generic “Process” section before reaching the evidence.

**On a deep read**, the case can reveal category-specific construction, iterations, credits, and specifications, but only when supported by a matching artifact or record. Internal limitations, capture requests, and claim states remain in the review record.

### Copy rule

Use the sequence **context → decision → visible result → consequence**. Avoid copy that merely names normal stages (“define, explore, refine, hand off”) or explains what a future production asset should contain. If no real production record exists, narrow the public scope to what the approved presentation actually shows and keep the proof backlog internal.

## 4. Category-specific modules

### A. Character and game-art cases

**Add or prioritize**

- Role and source: original concept, inherited concept, scan/base, personal/client/shipped status, and collaborators.
- Identity proof: hero plus neutral front, three-quarter, profile, and rear only when these views are from one matching asset or clearly labelled direction.
- Form decisions: anatomy, silhouette, costume construction, prop interaction, and one meaningful revision.
- Technical proof proportional to the title: high/low match, readable wireframe and loops, UVs, bake, texture sets or shader response, rig/deformation, engine view, and specifications when verified.
- For avatars or mascots, show application-specific evidence: thumbnail read, expression or viseme system, rig/tracking test, prop handling, or actual brand use.

**Omit until verified**

- “Game-ready,” “retopology,” “rig-ready,” “PBR,” “avatar-ready,” or engine language that is supported only by generated direction images.
- Faceted clay described as low-poly topology.
- Multiple neutral views that do not come from a consistent model or do not answer a new question.

**Adapt the backbone**

- Put role, source concept, target, and status beside the hero.
- Make the proof sequence follow the asset: brief/reference → blockout/form decision → construction → technical proof → final in target context.
- For a direction-only case, replace the technical sequence with identity locks, construction questions, and an explicit handoff brief; keep the title aligned to that narrower scope.

### B. Props and interiors

**Add or prioritize**

- Scale, use case, viewing distance, asset family, and whether the work is a single prop, kit, set dressing, or complete scene.
- Reference-to-design decisions and construction logic, not only material mood.
- Neutral blockout, scale comparisons, modular or repeated-part system, high/low, wireframe, UV/map strategy, material response, and scene organization when they exist.
- Engine, performance, collision, LOD, or export proof only when relevant and captured from the matching asset.
- One wide composition plus one or two close views tied to hierarchy, contact, material, or construction decisions.

**Omit until verified**

- “Production environment,” modularity, functional hardware, optimized, or game-ready claims inferred from a polished shelf render.
- Long prop lineups without scale, naming, grouping, or a reason the family exists.

**Adapt the backbone**

- Make the professional question system-oriented: how the objects share a construction and material language, how the set directs attention, or how the kit meets a target.
- Use the proof sequence reference/brief → blockout/scale → family or module logic → material/technical proof → final scene.

### C. Visual direction and client-review workflows

**Add or prioritize**

- Case status at the top: real client, internal, fictional, or reconstructed.
- The input that arrived, what was ambiguous, who owned the decision, and what Lesly owned.
- Two or more genuinely different options, annotated feedback, rejected direction, selected direction, decision rationale, version or date, responsible reviewer, and next action.
- Handoff artifact: a brief, approved board, change record, source link, delivery structure, or downstream result.
- Outcome: approved, paused, revised, implemented, handed off, or still proof-pending.

**Omit until verified**

- Client, approval, construction, implementation, measured-outcome, or site-verification language for fictional studies.
- AI language as the headline. The valuable skill is controlled visual decision-making and human-owned review; AI is supporting workflow context.
- Polished before/after images presented as an operational workflow when no review trace exists.

**Adapt the backbone**

- Put the decision trace at the center: input → options → review → decision → handoff → outcome.
- Use only enough final imagery to establish the direction. A real comment, draw-over, version comparison, or state record is more valuable than a third beauty board.

### D. Print and collectibles

**Add or prioritize**

- Scope and target: character or prop, intended scale, production process, personal/client/licensed status, and who owns sculpting, engineering, printing, assembly, painting, and photography.
- Digital form evidence: multiple angles, thickness-risk areas, base and center-of-gravity logic, and the decision that protects silhouette at physical scale.
- Engineering: actual part separation, keyed joints, tolerances, dimensions, minimum thickness, watertightness, hollowing/drainage, supports, and slicer view where relevant.
- Physical evidence: failed or revised test, successful print, part fit, assembly, surface cleanup, paint or final finish, and settings tied to the object.

**Omit until verified**

- “Print-ready,” “manufacturable,” “production parts,” or successful assembly claims based on proposed component groups.
- Exploded-looking presentation boards that do not show real keys, cut lines, tolerances, or source geometry.

**Adapt the backbone**

- Make the proof sequence concept/scope tradeoff → sculpt/form decision → engineering risk and parting → slicer/test → physical assembly/final.
- If no source model or test print exists, name the case collectible direction, keep the proposed assembly study, and close with the engineering proof still required.

## 5. Structured audit of Lesly's homepage and all 13 cases

### Portfolio-level and homepage findings

**Visible strengths**

- The homepage presents a clear role—3D Character Artist—with a prominent contact path and a visually cohesive body of work.
- All 13 registered public case IDs are represented, and the live heroes are visually distinct. No case needs to be removed to resolve duplication.
- The case pages avoid inventing clients, shipped credits, topology, UVs, source scenes, approvals, or print outcomes, but they over-explain those omissions to the public.
- The direction boards generally hold character identity, palette, material hierarchy, and presentation quality across multiple views.

**Structural weaknesses**

- The homepage headline “Production-ready 3D characters from concepts, sketches, and AI references” sets a stronger production claim than the currently visible technical evidence supports across the case set. **Recommended:** either surface matching production proof near the featured cases or narrow/qualify the claim.
- On case pages, the hero dominates the first viewport. Title, role, scope, ownership, project status, and evidence status arrive after the image rather than with it.
- Every family uses substantially the same long sequence and generic headings. This creates the appearance of thoroughness but makes the cases feel templated and delays the most valuable evidence.
- The current pages are often strongest as professional visual-direction studies. The copy acknowledges this, but titles and metadata sometimes imply a later production stage.
- The “Real capture required” content exposes the internal evidence backlog and should be removed from public pages. Narrow or omit unsupported claims and keep the ranked next capture in the internal record.

### Cross-case diagnosis

The 13 cases should remain public and distinct, but not all need to be equally long or make equally advanced production claims. The current assets can credibly support a portfolio of visual direction, character identity, staging, material hierarchy, costume logic, and evidence-aware handoff thinking. A smaller subset should become technical flagship cases only after matching source evidence exists.

## 6. Prioritized roadmap

### P0 — Correct first-read truth and prove the flagship claims

1. **Add a first-screen project-story contract to every case.** Required public fields: positive scope, supportable contribution, assignment, starting point, and finished presentation. Keep unknown role, ownership, status, and date fields out of public copy until documented.
2. **Align titles and homepage positioning to current evidence.** Technical terms such as production-ready, retopology, PBR, pipeline, progress, and print-ready must either have matching proof or be narrowed to direction, study, plan, or reconstruction.
3. **Choose 3–4 flagship cases for real proof capture.** Recommended first set: Forge Warden for character/game, Lyra or Marsh Warden for technical character proof, Ornate Interior for prop/environment proof, and Sol Collectible for print proof. Concentrate real source evidence instead of spreading aspirational technical copy across all 13.
4. **Remove public proof-debt architecture.** Do not expose evidence status, missing-proof narration, capture requests, or hypothetical production instructions. Narrow titles and claims, then tell a substantive finished story.
5. **Create an internal source-lineage record before adding proof.** For each image or capture: case ID, source asset, author/owner, tool or context, date/version if known, permission status, and the claim it can support. The public renderer must not consume this record.

**P0 is complete when:** a scan reader understands the project, contribution, and finished presentation without encountering audit language; every technical title has matching support or narrower wording; and unsupported technical claims are absent rather than publicly caveated.

### P1 — Differentiate the cases through judgment and category proof

1. **Give each case one professional question.** Examples: Can Lesly preserve character identity through a game-facing construction? Can she control a visual review without losing ownership? Can she turn collectible appeal into a testable physical object?
2. **Use category modules rather than one universal long template.** Character, environment, workflow, and print cases should have different evidence sequences and different hard blockers.
3. **Add one meaningful decision or tradeoff per case.** Use an annotated comparison, rejected option, movement risk, scale decision, material selection, camera selection, review comment, or manufacturing constraint. Do not invent one if no record exists.
4. **Make credits and handoffs explicit.** Especially for client work, sourced concepts, team production, vendor printing, or downstream execution.
5. **Use current generated boards as presentation evidence, not technical evidence.** They can remain valuable hero, identity, staging, material, and direction assets when captioned accurately.

**P1 is complete when:** each case has a distinct reason to exist, its evidence matches its category, and no two cases depend on the same generic process narrative.

### P2 — Strengthen outcomes, recruiter scanability, and comparative quality

1. **Add verified outcomes where they exist:** shipped, approved, delivered, adopted, paused, declined, test-printed, or learned. An honest non-success outcome can still demonstrate senior judgment.
2. **Add measurable specifications only from matching records.** Counts, texture sets, scale, print settings, dates, version states, and tool contexts should be source-backed.
3. **Improve visual captions and accessibility.** A caption should say what changed, why it mattered, and what the image proves. Ensure crops, mobile order, alt text, and contrast preserve that meaning.
4. **Review the portfolio as a set.** Featured order should move from strongest production proof to differentiated direction skills, with visually distinct related-case recommendations and no repeated hero family.
5. **Re-benchmark annually or after major proof capture.** The standard should move when real source evidence changes, not when more prose is added.

**P2 is complete when:** flagship cases can withstand detailed recruiter scrutiny, supporting cases remain concise and honest, and the portfolio communicates both craft and professional accountability.

## 9. Recommendations for `build-lesly-case-study`

These are read-only recommendations. No skill file was changed as part of this audit.

### Keep

- One-case-at-a-time orchestration and preservation of every registered case ID.
- The current separation between generated presentation media and production truth.
- Independent craft, copy, and browser review.
- The prohibition on inventing clients, technical evidence, outcomes, or source facts.
- Distinct hero and supporting media requirements, with duplicate-family checks.

### Strengthen

1. **Add a case-story contract before asset work.** Internally require status, role, ownership, input/source, output, team, date, professional question, strongest evidenced claim, and highest-risk unsupported claim. Publicly expose only documented facts and a positive supportable scope. If contribution or permission cannot be established, stop rather than publish an unknown/unverified field.
2. **Route the case to one category module.** Character/game, props/interiors, visual-direction/client-review, or print/collectible should each have different proof requirements and hard blockers.
3. **Make first-10-second and first-30-second browser gates explicit.** The reviewer should confirm that result, title, positive scope, supportable contribution, and assignment are visible immediately; then that problem, decision, consequence, and strongest visible result are clear within a short scroll.
4. **Replace a fixed media count with proof slots.** “One hero plus two supporting visuals” is a useful default, but it can force decorative boards. Require one hero and the minimum number of images needed to answer the professional question. A second support image should exist only if it proves something new.
5. **Add an internal source-lineage manifest.** Every media item should record source path, source owner, generation or capture type, permission, matching-asset status, and supported claim. Check it before copy is written; never render its field names or missing states publicly.
6. **Add claim-specific hard blockers.** Examples: no retopology title without matching wireframe; no PBR technical claim without matching maps/shader evidence; no client workflow without a real permissioned decision record; no print-ready claim without engineering and physical test evidence. Hard blockers should override an aggregate quality score.
7. **Require one decision or tradeoff, not generic process coverage.** The case must show a supportable constraint, choice, and consequence. If no such record exists, stop or use only a visibly demonstrable design decision; do not publish the absence.
8. **Make role and credits a mandatory copy gate.** The skill should fail closed on ambiguous ownership, inherited concepts or bases, team output, vendor printing, internal-team completion, or reviewer responsibility.
9. **Separate presentation certification from outcome certification.** Craft review can approve a generated hero as professional direction media. It cannot approve topology, game readiness, workflow operation, print engineering, client approval, or a measured outcome.
10. **Add category-specific next-capture ranking to the internal review record.** The public page must not name or request the capture.
11. **Treat the 92/100 gate as secondary to hard truth boundaries.** A polished case with a missing source, ambiguous role, or unsupported title should not pass because other rubric categories compensate numerically.
12. **Add set-level review after individual approval.** Confirm that the case has a distinct professional question, hero family, evidence type, and recruiter value relative to the other 12 cases. Preservation does not require equal prominence or identical length.

### Suggested skill outputs per completed case

- one independent craft review;

### Final decision rule

A case is ready for public presentation when its visual quality is professional **and** a scan reader can accurately understand the project, Lesly's supportable contribution, the decisions, the visible result, and the professional value without encountering an audit or proof-debt narrative. It is ready to make a technical or outcome claim only when matching source evidence exists; otherwise that claim is omitted.
